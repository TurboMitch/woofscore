Best dog food for a American Hairless Terrier

The American Hairless Terrier is a small breed (about 4–12 kg, Terrier group). Here's what actually matters in its diet — and the foods that fit.

What a American Hairless Terrier needs in its food

  • Appropriately small kibble
  • Calorie-dense but portion-controlled food — small dogs are easy to overfeed
  • Dental-friendly food and chews
  • Skin & coat: they can be prone to skin/coat issues and food sensitivities — prioritise named fish or fish oil (omega-3 EPA/DHA), a single named protein, and a limited-ingredient recipe if itchy.
  • Dental health: small mouths build tartar quickly — prioritise appropriately small kibble, dental chews, and skipping sugary treats.

What to look for on the label

Look for small kibble, a named protein first and moderate calories; named fish or fish oil (omega-3 EPA/DHA), a single named protein, and a limited-ingredient recipe if itchy; appropriately small kibble, dental chews, and skipping sugary treats.

A note on “American Hairless Terrier formula” foods

Many brands sell a breed-specific “American Hairless Terrier” food. These are often vet-formulated and perfectly fine — but a breed name on the bag is mostly shape and marketing. Frequently asked questions

What is the best dog food for a American Hairless Terrier?

There isn't one single best food, but for a small breed the priorities are clear: small kibble, a named protein first and moderate calories; named fish or fish oil (omega-3 EPA/DHA), a single named protein, and a limited-ingredient recipe if itchy; appropriately small kibble, dental chews, and skipping sugary treats. The WoofScore-rated options above all score well on ingredient quality and suit a American Hairless Terrier's needs. Always confirm with your vet, especially for puppies, seniors or dogs with health conditions.

How much should a American Hairless Terrier eat?

Feed to your dog's ideal body weight, not the maximum on the bag — a small breed (about 4–12 kg) needs far fewer calories than a larger dog. Split into two meals, measure portions, and adjust so you can easily feel (but not see) the ribs. Your vet can set a target weight.

Is grain-free better for this breed?

Usually no. Unless your dog has a diagnosed grain allergy, whole grains like brown rice and oats are healthy. Grain-free diets heavy in peas/legumes have been linked by the FDA to a possible heart issue (DCM), so most vets don't recommend grain-free without a medical reason.
